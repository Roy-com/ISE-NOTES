Frequently Asked Questions

1. What is the Industrial & Systems Engineering core curriculum like?

Industrial and Systems are the two terms that come to mind. The goal of engineering is to improve the efficiency of an industry's operations. It's more of a crossroads between Computer Science and Management. Product Development, Process Planning, and Operation Resources are taught on one hand, and Python, Optimizing Codes, and Procedural Programming are taught on the other. We have a couple of Mechanical Department courses as well, but only the fascinating and applied ones, not the fundamental, theoretical mechanical courses. We have a lot of term projects, so we get to do a lot of hands-on learning and research.

2. What is the structure/schedule of the course? Which semesters necessitate more work and attention? How difficult is it to maintain a respectable grade point average while participating in extracurricular activities?

The programme is laid-back and unhurried. It is possible to engage in extracurricular activities while keeping a good CGPA. Probability and statistics are heavily emphasised in the curriculum. There are some theory classes available, but not many. The majority of courses are mathematical, logical, or analytical in nature. The third semester is the busiest of them all. However, during your second year, you'll have a better sense of what's going on and how to plan properly. The fundamental courses appear in the 5th and 6th semesters and are simple to comprehend and pass.

3. What are the research areas in IM? What are some reputable MS/Ph.D. programmes that accept freshmen around the world?

MS programmes in Industrial & Systems Engineering have a lot of potential in the United States. In reality, it is on a par with Computer Science and Electrical Engineering research. Industrial is also a chosen branch for study in general. Our curriculum is heavily influenced by Georgia Institute of Technology's Industrial and Systems Engineering programme, which is regarded as one of the best in the country. As a result, our curriculum improves our chances of getting included in the transcript. Math, Data Analytics, Relative Maintenance, and Supply Chain Management are among the most common research topics.

4. What are the job options for someone who is interested in the field but does not want to pursue an MS/Ph.D. after college? What are the most important courses for CDC Internships and Placements?

There are some companies in CDC that are available expressly for Chemical, Mechanical, and Industrial graduates, but they tend to favour chemical and mechanical grads more than us, and they especially overlook students with poor CGPAs. Core supply chain businesses, ITC, and P&G are just a few of them.

These businesses offer supply chain management and product development projects, which are ideal for a curriculum like ours, but unfortunately, these businesses haven't yet formed this understanding, as seen by the fact that they didn't open for Industrial until a year ago. As a result, I wouldn't put too much faith in CDC because the prospects are limited. There is a glimpse of optimism with the emergence of companies like Udaan, but the future remains uncertain. Getting an FT is the best option.

Probability and Statistics is the most significant topic, however other areas such as Information Systems and Operations Research are also covered. People are mostly recruited into open to everyone enterprises or software sectors, as I've noticed. As a result, the core prospects for core profiles in CDC are limited.

5. What degree of adaptability is there in the curriculum? What courses should you choose for a minor or micro, and how should you supplement your core curriculum with subjects from other departments?

Because we don't have a lot of subject-based assignments like Computer Science or Electrical Engineering, most students in our department take additionals from other departments. This gives us a lot of time to do other things. We don't even receive a lot of lab work. Everything is done largely in the lab and takes very little time. The majority of these assignments are done in groups.

People usually take minor/micro-courses from the Departments of Computer Science and Mathematics. If you're doing a four-year programme, you'll need to put in a lot of extra time to finish CSE minors. The Maths minor, on the other hand, is less difficult to finish. If you're doing a dual degree, you won't have any problems because you'll have plenty of time in your fourth and fifth years to complete minor curricular courses.

6. What is the CDC intern's situation in the Industrial Department? How difficult is it to get a full-time position at a reputable university?

Securing an FT is largely on on individual initiative; the department does not have a significant role in the process. It's more about the projects you choose and how you complete them in a foreign programme. Foreign academics typically take into account your past project experience, CG, and they place a high value on perseverance. On average, 10–15 percent of a batch's participants receive an FT from our department.

7. How does the Dual Degree programme differ from the B.Tech. programme?

It's not that dissimilar. A Dual Degree course does not add to your knowledge in any substantial way. It's more about you getting an extra year to do some extracurricular activities and find out what you want to do. It's not like an M.Tech, where you may study a subject in depth. One thing to remember is that while a BTP can be completed in any department and on any project of your choice, an MTP must be completed within one's own department. This is significant since the MTPs and BTPs have close to 25 credits and are extremely essential academically.

If you want to learn more and go deeper, I recommend getting an MS instead of converting to a Dual Degree. However, one significant advantage of the Dual Degree programme is that in your fifth year, the stipend you get covers practically all of your academic expenses.
